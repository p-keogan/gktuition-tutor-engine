## üß∞ The applications of integration (Int 8‚Äì13)

If asked *"what are the applications of integration?"*, give the **full** set ‚Äî not just find-`f(x)` and kinematics:

- **Finding `f(x)` from `f'(x)`** ‚Äî anti-differentiate + pin `+ C` with an initial condition (Int 8)
- **Kinematics** ‚Äî acceleration ‚Üí velocity ‚Üí position (Int 9)
- **Anti-derivatives** ‚Äî differentiate-then-rearrange (Int 10)
- **Average (mean) value of a function** ‚Äî `(1/(b‚àía))¬∑‚à´‚Çê·µá f(x) dx` (Int 11) ‚Äî *not in the tables; memorise it*
- **Area between a curve and the x-axis** (Int 12) and **the y-axis** (Int 13)

> **Average value and area under a curve are core applications ‚Äî never omit them.** Both are regularly examined (area every year; average value 6√ó in 9 recent years).

## üìö Learning work ‚Äî what must be in your head before you sit down

These are the items the log tables **don't** give you. Drill until they're automatic.

### 1. The average-value formula

```
                  1     ‚åÝ b
   Average  =  ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ ‚îÇ   f(x) dx
                b ‚àí a  ‚å° a
```

> Paul at `integration-11` [01:09]: *"it's not available in your maths table so you need to commit that to memory."* The most common error: students compute the definite integral and call **that** the average. The `1/(b‚àía)` factor is what makes it an average, not a total. (2023 P1 Q7(d) MS explicit: *"If 1/5 is omitted, treat step 1 as not fully correct."*)

### 2. The divide-by-derivative-of-power trick

For `‚à´ e^(kx) dx` (where `k` is a constant):

```
   ‚åÝ
   ‚îÇ  e^(kx) dx  =  (1/k) e^(kx)  +  C
   ‚å°
```

Same pattern for trig: `‚à´ sin(kx) dx = ‚àí(1/k) cos(kx) + C`. The mechanic is the inverse of the chain rule from Diff 5. It is **load-bearing** when `k = ‚àí1` because the sign of the antiderivative flips.

### 3. `+ C` discipline ‚Äî indefinite vs definite

> **Indefinite integrals: `+ C` is mandatory** (Paul reinforces this 30+ times in `integration-1` alone ‚Äî it's the most common LC trap).
>
> **Definite integrals: `+ C` is gone.** `(F(b) + C) ‚àí (F(a) + C) = F(b) ‚àí F(a)` ‚Äî the constants cancel. Writing `+ C` in a definite-integral answer typically costs 1 mark per occurrence.

### 4. There is NO product / quotient / chain rule for integration in LC

> Paul at `integration-1` [05:58]: *"there's no quotient rule or product rule or chain rule in integration in the Leaving Cert."*

If the integrand is a fraction with `x` in both numerator and denominator, you must **factorise and cancel** before integrating. If it's a product, you must **expand or simplify** first. There is no direct rule.

## üö® LOAD-BEARING ‚Äî the five things that win or lose the integration question

### 1. The signed-area trap ‚Äî split at every interior root

> Definite integration gives a **signed** quantity. Above-axis regions contribute positive; below-axis regions contribute negative. They can cancel.

Paul at `integration-12` [11:25] (delivered as a triple mantra at [08:02], [11:25], [12:06]): *"if I had integrated from 0 to 4 your answer would have ended up being 0."*

**The recipe** for area between `y = f(x)` and the x-axis between `x = a` and `x = b`:
1. **Sketch first** ‚Äî mandatory. Identify where the curve crosses the x-axis between `a` and `b`.
2. **Split the integral at every interior root.** Each region gets its own integral.
3. **Take the absolute value** of each region's integral before summing.

> üö® This is the **single biggest mark-loss** in integration. The student integrates from `a` to `b` in one go, the positive and negative areas cancel, and the answer is zero or wildly wrong. Tested 2017, 2018, 2022, 2023, 2024, 2024 DF, 2025 DF.

### 2. The average-value formula ‚Äî memorise it

> `avg = (1/(b‚àía)) ¬∑ ‚à´‚Çê·µá f(x) dx` ‚Äî **not in the log tables**.

The two-step structure of the question is almost always:
1. Integrate `f(x)` from `a` to `b`.
2. **Divide by `(b‚àía)`.**

> üö® Paul at `integration-11` [01:09]: *"it's not available in your maths table so you need to commit that to memory."* The MS deducts marks specifically for omitting the `1/(b‚àía)` factor. Tested 2017, 2020, 2023 (avg speed via integration), 2024, 2024 DF, 2025.

### 3. `+ C` is mandatory for indefinite integrals; pinned by initial condition

Indefinite integrals must include `+ C`. When a question gives an initial condition (e.g. *"the curve passes through (1, 5)"*, or *"the particle has velocity 3 m/s at t = 0"*), **substitute to find `C`**, then write the full particular function.

**The applications-strand recipe** (Int 8): integrate the rate to get the function, then sub the boundary condition to pin `C`.

> üö® Tested every year. The two ways students lose marks: (a) forgetting `+ C` on an indefinite integral; (b) finding `C` but then forgetting to write the full pinned function. Tested 2019, 2021, 2024 DF, 2025.

### 4. Definite-integral mechanic ‚Äî sub into the antiderivative, not the integrand

> `‚à´‚Çê·µá f(x) dx = [F(x)]‚Çê·µá = F(b) ‚àí F(a)`

Sub `a` and `b` into the **antiderivative** `F(x)`, not into the original `f(x)`. (Same pattern as the Diff 14/15 mark-losing trap, running in reverse.) For trig integrals with `œÄ`-limits the calculator must be in **radian mode** ‚Äî Paul at `integration-7` [04:02] flags this explicitly.

> üö® Tested 11/11 years ‚Äî most-cited integration tutorial in the corpus (16 citations).

### 5. Simplify the integrand BEFORE you integrate

> There is no product, quotient, or chain rule in LC integration. If the integrand isn't a standard form on page 26, your first move is **algebraic simplification**.

Common simplifications:
- Split a fraction: `‚à´(x¬≤ + 3)/x dx = ‚à´(x + 3/x) dx = x¬≤/2 + 3 ln|x| + C`.
- Expand a product: `‚à´(2x + 1)(x ‚àí 3) dx` ‚Üí multiply out, then integrate term by term.
- Factor and cancel: `‚à´(x¬≤ ‚àí 9)/(x ‚àí 3) dx = ‚à´(x + 3) dx`.

> üö® Paul at `integration-1` [05:58]: *"there's no quotient rule or product rule or chain rule in integration in the Leaving Cert."* If the integrand looks complicated, simplify first. Tested every year.

## üéØ The 8 techniques you must execute without thinking

1. **Power rule for integration**: `‚à´ x‚Åø dx = x‚Åø‚Å∫¬π/(n+1) + C` for `n ‚âÝ ‚àí1`. Add one to the power; divide by the new power.
2. **The `n = ‚àí1` edge case**: `‚à´ (1/x) dx = ln|x| + C`. Not a power-rule result ‚Äî a separate antiderivative.
3. **Exponential with linear inside**: `‚à´ e^(kx) dx = (1/k) e^(kx) + C`. The "divide by the derivative of the power" trick. (Int 3.)
4. **`‚à´ aÀ£ dx = aÀ£/(ln a) + C`** for constant base `a ‚âÝ e`. Different formula from `‚à´eÀ£`. Page 26.
5. **Trig integration with linear inside**: `‚à´ sin(kx) dx = ‚àí(1/k) cos(kx) + C`; `‚à´ cos(kx) dx = (1/k) sin(kx) + C`. Same divide-by-derivative trick. (Int 5.)
6. **Product-to-sum before trig integration**: when integrating `sin A ¬∑ cos B` etc., use the page-15 product-to-sum identities to convert to single-term sines/cosines first. (Int 6.)
7. **Definite-integral evaluation**: write the antiderivative in square brackets with the limits, then evaluate at upper minus lower. `[F(x)]‚Çê·µá = F(b) ‚àí F(a)`. (Int 7.)
8. **Find f(x) from f'(x) + initial condition**: integrate, get `f(x) + C`, sub the initial point, solve for `C`. (Int 8.)

## ‚öÝ Common traps ‚Äî where students lose marks

| Trap | Fix |
|---|---|
| Forgetting `+ C` on an indefinite integral | Always add `+ C`. Paul drills 30+ times in Int 1. |
| Writing `+ C` on a definite integral | The `C`s cancel: `(F(b) + C) ‚àí (F(a) + C) = F(b) ‚àí F(a)`. |
| Integrating across an interior root without splitting (signed-area trap) | Sketch first. Split at every crossing. Take absolute values. Sum. |
| Average-value answer = the integral (forgot to divide by `b ‚àí a`) | The `1/(b‚àía)` factor is mandatory. (2023 P1 Q7(d) MS: *"if 1/5 is omitted, treat step 1 as not fully correct."*) |
| Substituting limits into `f(x)` instead of `F(x)` | Sub into the **antiderivative**, not the integrand |
| Calculator in degrees for trig integrals with `œÄ`-limits | Switch to **radians**. Paul flags at Int 7 [04:02]. |
| Wrong sign on `‚à´ sin x = +cos x` (should be `‚àícos x`) | `d/dx[cos x] = ‚àísin x`, so reversing it: `‚à´ sin x dx = ‚àícos x + C` |
| Forgetting the `(1/k)` on `‚à´ e^(kx) dx` | Divide by the derivative of the linear-in-x in the exponent. `k = ‚àí1` flips the sign. |
| Trying a quotient/product/chain rule on the integrand | There is no such rule. Factorise / expand / cancel first. |
| Wrong page-26 row: using `‚à´aÀ£ = aÀ£/(ln a)` for `‚à´eÀ£` | `‚à´eÀ£ = eÀ£ + C` ‚Äî `ln e = 1` so the formulas agree; but don't write `ln e` in the denominator |
| Area answer left as a negative number | Area is positive. Take absolute value of any below-axis region. |
| Computing area between two curves with `‚à´(top ‚àí bottom)` when one is below the x-axis | When the curves are on opposite sides of the x-axis, compute each enclosed region separately and sum magnitudes. (Int 12 Q4.) |

## üí° Three exam-day tips that move the needle

1. **Sketch every area-under-the-curve question, no exceptions.** Without a sketch you cannot see whether the curve dips below the x-axis between your limits ‚Äî and that is the single biggest mark-loss in integration. Paul drills this triple-mantra style in Int 12 [08:02], [11:25], [12:06] ‚Äî when he repeats himself three times, it's exam-critical.

2. **The average-value formula is the one you must memorise.** Page 26 has every standard antiderivative; it does **not** have `avg = (1/(b‚àía)) ¬∑ ‚à´‚Çê·µá f(x) dx`. Write it from memory on the cover of your answer book as soon as the exam starts, before anything else.

3. **Indefinite ‚áí `+ C`; definite ‚áí no `+ C`.** Indefinite vs definite is the first thing to identify in every integration question. Indefinite always gets `+ C`. Definite never does. This binary choice catches 1‚Äì2 marks per paper on average.
