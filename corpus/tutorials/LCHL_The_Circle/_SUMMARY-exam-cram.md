## 🧭 Why The Circle deserves the first 90 minutes

From the 11-year exam-trends analysis (2015–2025, 30 papers, 1,212 tagged question-parts):

- **The Circle appears in 11/11 years on Paper 2.** Average ≈ 3.0 parts per paper. Total: **33 P2 parts**.
- **Recent surge:** 2024 had **6** circle parts; 2025 had **5**. Prior 9-year average was ≈ 2.4. Plan as if 2026 keeps the heavier weighting.
- **Section A heavy** — 25 of 33 parts (~76%) sit in Section A. Circle is mostly tested as bounded, ~6-minute mechanics questions, not as the spine of a Section B context.
- **Cross-strand reach.** Algebra fires inside every Circle question through the quadratic formula (line-circle intersections), the discriminant (tangency tests), and simultaneous equations. The Line is a permanent partner — slope, midpoint, distance, perpendicular distance from a point to a line.

**The takeaway.** Circle is the most predictable strand on Paper 2: the same handful of moves recycles every year. Marks lost here are easy marks lost, not hard ones — almost all of it reduces to *find the centre, find the radius, then apply one rule*.

## 📚 Learning work — what must be in your head before you sit down

These are the items the log tables **don't** give you. Drill until automatic.

### 1. The 4-scenario two-circle classification

Compare `|C₁C₂|` (distance between centres) against `R₁ + R₂` and `|R₁ − R₂|`:

- `|C₁C₂| = R₁ + R₂` → **touch externally** (1 contact point, side by side)
- `|C₁C₂| = |R₁ − R₂|` → **touch internally** (1 contact point, one inside the other)
- `|R₁ − R₂| < |C₁C₂| < R₁ + R₂` → **intersect** (2 contact points)
- `|C₁C₂| > R₁ + R₂` → **disjoint** (0 contact points)

> Memorise these four. The question wording (*"prove they are touching"*, *"find the common chord"*) tells you which to verify.

### 2. The three axes-as-tangent rules

- **x-axis tangent** ⟹ `R = |−f|` ⟹ `c = g²`
- **y-axis tangent** ⟹ `R = |−g|` ⟹ `c = f²`
- **Both axes tangent** ⟹ `|−g| = |−f| = R`; centre has form `(k, k)`, `(k, −k)`, `(−k, k)`, or `(−k, −k)`

### 3. Tangent ⊥ radius (Theorem 19)

> Slope of tangent = **negative reciprocal** of the slope of the radius drawn to the point of contact. Not the reciprocal — the negative reciprocal. Product of perpendicular slopes is `−1`.

### 4. The discriminant trichotomy for line meeting circle

Sub the line into the circle, get a quadratic, look at `b² − 4ac`:

- `> 0` → 2 distinct intersections → **chord**
- `= 0` → 1 repeated root → **tangent**
- `< 0` → no real roots → **miss**

## 🚨 LOAD-BEARING — the five things that win or lose the circle question

### 1. Centre `(−g, −f)`, radius `√(g² + f² − c)` from Form 2

The single most-tested fact in the entire strand. Tested **every year**.

| Form 2 equation | Centre | Radius |
|---|---|---|
| `x² + y² + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0` | `(−g, −f)` | `√(g² + f² − c)` |

> 🚨 **The marks-rule.** When substituting into `R = √(g² + f² − c)`, substitute **`g` and `f`**, NOT `−g` and `−f`. For centre `(−6, 2)`: `g = 6`, `f = −2`. Paul (`the-circle-1`): *"students often get lazy and they realise it doesn't matter what sign you have in your bracket if you're squaring it but it does matter for getting the marks."* Squaring hides the sign in the numerical answer; the marking scheme does not.

### 2. Rearrange to `= 0` BEFORE reading off coefficients

> 🚨 If the equation is presented as `x² + y² − 3x − 5y = 9` or `2x² + 2y² + ... = 0`, you must **move all terms to one side** and **divide by the leading coefficient** before extracting `2g`, `2f`, `c`.

Same rule, two flavours: (a) move RHS to LHS; (b) normalise leading coefficient to 1. Paul (`the-circle-3`): *"the coefficients of x squared and y squared are 2. So before you can find the centre or the radius, you have to divide everything by 2."* Reading coefficients off an un-rearranged or un-normalised equation gives the wrong centre AND the wrong radius. Tested 2022 P2, 2024 P2, recurring.

### 3. Tangent is perpendicular to the radius at the point of contact

> **`slope_tangent = −1 / slope_radius`** when the question gives a point ON the circle.

The recipe: (1) find the centre; (2) compute slope of radius from centre to point; (3) take the negative reciprocal; (4) point-slope `y − y₁ = m(x − x₁)` using the tangent slope and the **given point** (not the centre).

> 🚨 **FROM vs AT — read the preposition.** *"tangent **at** the point"* → 1 tangent, point is ON the circle, use perp-to-radius (`the-circle-7`). *"tangents **from** the point"* → 2 tangents, point is OUTSIDE, use generic-slope + perpendicular-distance-equals-radius (`the-circle-8`). Same-shaped wording, completely different machinery. Tested 2015, 2022, 2023, 2024, 2025.

### 4. Set `K₁ = K₂` to find the common chord / common tangent

> 🚨 **Two-line algebra.** For two circles in standard Form 2 (both leading coefficient 1), subtract one equation from the other. The `x²` and `y²` terms cancel, leaving a **linear** equation in `x` and `y` — that line is the common chord (if circles intersect) or the common tangent (if circles touch). Paul (`the-circle-5`): *"It's an incredibly easy question that we can carry out in almost two or three lines."*

If circles touch, sub the resulting line back into either circle — the substituted quadratic factors as a perfect square `(...)² = 0` (one repeated root), which **confirms** the tangency. Tested 2015, 2018, 2020, 2023, 2024 DF.

### 5. The two-answer awareness on axis-as-tangent questions

> 🚨 *"Find the circle with the y-axis as tangent at `(0, −5)`"* has **two answers** — one circle to the right (centre `(R, −5)`), one to the left (centre `(−R, −5)`). Write both equations unless the question pins the quadrant (*"in the first quadrant"*, *"centre with positive x-coordinate"*). Marking schemes expect both. Same logic for tangents-parallel-to-a-line: two parallel tangents per direction, one on each side of the circle.

## 🎯 The 7 techniques you must execute without thinking

1. **Write the equation given centre + radius (Form 1).** `(x − h)² + (y − k)² = R²`. Don't multiply out the LHS; do compute the RHS. *"You are not expected and you don't have to multiply out the left hand side ... you ARE expected to multiply 6 by 6."* (`the-circle-1`).
2. **Extract centre via the divide-by-(−2) shortcut.** `x-coord of centre = (coeff of x) ÷ (−2)`. Same for y. Faster than the long-form `2g = ... → g = ... → −g = ...`.
3. **Sub a point into the equation to test ON-circle.** Substitute the point; if LHS = 0 (Form 2) or LHS = RHS (Form 1), it lies on the circle. Works even without normalising leading coefficients.
4. **Distance-to-centre vs radius for inside/outside.** Compute `d = √((x − x_c)² + (y − y_c)²)`. `d < R` → inside; `d = R` → on; `d > R` → outside. Compare **in surd form** — *"Square root of 17 is clearly smaller than the square root of 20"* (`the-circle-3`). Don't decimalise.
5. **Line into circle → substitute → quadratic.** Isolate the variable with coefficient 1 from the line; substitute into the circle; collect into a quadratic; discriminant tells you chord/tangent/miss. For a chord, solve the quadratic for both roots, then **back-substitute into the line** (not the circle) for the other coordinate.
6. **Tangent from an external point — the generic-slope move.** Write the unknown line as `y − y₁ = m(x − x₁)` with `m` symbolic. Set perpendicular-distance-from-centre = radius. Square both sides to kill the modulus and the `√(m² + 1)` simultaneously. The resulting quadratic in `m` gives two slopes → two tangents. Both are valid.
7. **Circle through three points (Form 2).** Substitute each point into `x² + y² + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0` to get three linear equations in `(g, f, c)`. Solve by elimination — pick equations with zero coordinates first (they eliminate a variable for free). Form 1 produces quadratics in `(h, k)`; **always use Form 2**.

## ⚠ Common traps — where students lose marks

| Trap | Fix |
|---|---|
| Reading `2g, 2f, c` off an un-rearranged equation | Move all terms to `= 0` first, then divide by leading coefficient if needed |
| Substituting `(−g)²` into `R = √(g² + f² − c)` | Use **`g`** (not `−g`); marks-rule even though squaring hides the sign |
| Mis-reading *"at"* vs *"from"* on tangent questions | *At* = 1 tangent (perp-to-radius); *from* = 2 tangents (perp-dist = R) |
| Writing just `+√k` for `m² = k` (or any squared unknown) | Square roots are `±`. Two tangents → two slopes; expect both |
| Reciprocal instead of negative reciprocal for perpendicular slope | Product of perp slopes = `−1`. Flip AND change sign |
| Forgetting the second answer on single-axis-tangent questions | Two circles — centre left/right or above/below the tangent axis |
| Computing radius when only the centre is needed (tangent at a point) | Only the centre is needed for slope-of-radius; the radius's length is irrelevant |
| Decimalising surds when comparing distance to radius | Keep `√17 < √20` in surd form; marking scheme rewards exact form |
| Skipping the concluding sentence on inside/outside proofs | Always state *"d > R, therefore the point lies outside the circle K"* |
| Sub line into circle for back-substitution | Back-sub into the **line** (linear → one y per x); subbing into circle re-introduces both roots |
| Three-point circle attempted with Form 1 | Form 1 gives quadratics in `(h, k)`. Always use Form 2 — linear in `(g, f, c)` |

## 📋 Question-type triage — reading the question wording

| Phrase | Strategy |
|---|---|
| *"…centre and radius of the circle…"* | Extract from Form 2 via page 19; rearrange/normalise first |
| *"Find the equation of the circle with centre ... and radius ..."* | Form 1; don't multiply out |
| *"Prove the point lies on the circle"* | Substitute the point into the equation (fastest; no normalisation needed) |
| *"Prove the point is inside / outside the circle"* | Distance-to-centre vs radius; compare in surd form |
| *"Find the tangent at the point…"* | Slope of radius → negative reciprocal → point-slope (`the-circle-7`) |
| *"Find the tangents from the external point…"* | Generic slope + perpendicular-distance = radius; **two answers** (`the-circle-8`) |
| *"Find the tangents parallel to the line…"* | Same `(a, b)`, unknown `c`; perp-distance = R; `±` produces two lines |
| *"Find the length of the tangent from…"* | Pythagoras on a right triangle (right angle at point of contact); one line of working |
| *"Show that the circles touch"* | Compute `|C₁C₂|`, compare to `R₁ ± R₂` |
| *"Find the common chord / common tangent"* | `K₁ = K₂` → the x² and y² cancel → linear equation |
| *"…passes through these three points…"* | Substitute each into Form 2 → 3 linear equations in `(g, f, c)` |
| *"…has the x-axis (or y-axis) as a tangent…"* | Apply `R = \|−f\|` or `R = \|−g\|`; expect two answers unless quadrant is fixed |
| *"…line intersects the circle at…"* / *"…is a chord/tangent/miss…"* | Substitute, get quadratic, read discriminant |

## 💡 Three exam-day tips that move the needle

1. **Open page 19 before reading the question.** Form 1, Form 2, centre `(−g, −f)`, radius `√(g² + f² − c)` are all there. Don't burn working memory on formulas the log tables hand you.

2. **Sketch every problem.** Two circles, axes-as-tangent, chord-on-an-axis, line-meets-circle — all live or die on the picture. Paul (`the-circle-3`): *"as we go through this topic, you're going to need to be drawing diagrams to try and figure out what's going on."* A 30-second sketch tells you the answer before the algebra finishes.

3. **Show centre → radius → equation, even when the question only asks for the equation.** Three lines, three method marks. The marking scheme rewards visible extraction of `(−g, −f)` and the explicit `R = √(g² + f² − c)` substitution.

## 🔗 Cross-strand connections (where else Circle fires)

- **Circle ↔ The Line** — every tangent question uses slope, point-slope, perpendicular distance from page 18; every circle-on-a-chord-as-diameter uses midpoint + distance.
- **Circle ↔ Algebra** — line-circle substitution produces a quadratic; tangent-from-external-point produces a quadratic in `m`; discriminant decides chord/tangent/miss. **16 cross-strand P2 citations** with Algebra in the 11-year window.
- **Circle ↔ Trigonometry 1** — tangent length from external point is a right-angled-triangle Pythagoras; chord-on-axis questions close with `R² = (half-chord)² + (perp distance)²`.
- **Circle ↔ Geometry 1** — Theorem 19 (tangent ⊥ radius) is the rule that powers `the-circle-7`; some Section B questions stitch a synthetic-geometry proof onto a coordinate-circle setup.

> Circle is "the most predictable strand." Almost every part is one of: extract centre and radius; apply tangent ⊥ radius; classify two circles by `|C₁C₂|`; substitute a line and read the discriminant.
