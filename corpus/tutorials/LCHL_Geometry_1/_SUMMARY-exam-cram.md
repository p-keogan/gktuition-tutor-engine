> **For students who already know this material.** This sheet is the triage map for your final 90 minutes on Synthetic Geometry before the exam. Read in 5 minutes, drill the priorities for 85.

## 🧭 Why Geometry deserves the first 90 minutes

From the 11-year exam-trends analysis (2015–2025, 30 papers, 1,212 tagged question-parts):

- **Geometry 1 appears in 11/11 years on Paper 2.** Average ≈ 3.5 parts per paper. Total: **38 P2 parts**.
- **Section A heavy** (27/38 in Section A). Geometry is mostly tested as proofs + construction-based short-form questions, with similar-triangles applications spilling into Section B.
- **No 2024–25 surge — steady weighting.** 2024 had **5** parts and 2025 had **4**, very close to the historical average. Do NOT expect a heavier hit in 2026; do expect the same recurring patterns (similar triangles + one prescribed proof) you've seen for a decade.
- **Cross-strand reach:** Similar triangles is the **single most-applied technique on Paper 2** — it fires inside AVM cone/sphere problems, inside The Circle (tangent-radius right triangles), inside Trig 1 (Pythagoras lives or dies on similar triangles), and inside coordinate geometry (centroid/circumcentre constructions).

**The takeaway.** Geometry is small in part-count but **structurally load-bearing**: marks lost on similar triangles cascade through three other P2 strands. And one of the three prescribed LC proofs is worth ~10 marks in Section A whenever it appears.

## 📚 Learning work — what must be in your head before you sit down

These are the items the log tables **don't** give you. Drill until they're automatic.

### 1. The four-piece vocabulary — Axiom, Theorem, Corollary, Converse

Memorise the definitions. A question CAN ask "define a converse" or "give an example of a statement whose converse is not true." Paul's canonical prepared answer: *"All rectangles are parallelograms"* is TRUE; *"all parallelograms are rectangles"* is FALSE. (geometry-1-1, content_warning #2.)

### 2. The three LC-required proofs — verbatim

| Theorem | One-line statement | Construction (the load-bearing step) |
|---|---|---|
| **T11** | 3 parallel lines cut equal segments on any transversal | Two auxiliary parallel lines → three parallelograms + ASA congruence |
| **T12** | Line parallel to a triangle's side cuts the other two sides in the same ratio | Divide AX into s parts, XB into t parts; draw parallels; apply T11 |
| **T13** | Similar triangles ⇒ sides proportional in order | Construct sub-triangle AXY on △ABC; SAS congruence with △DEF; apply T12 |

The five JC proofs (Theorems 4, 6, 9, 14, 19) are technically on the LC course but Paul flags they are *"very unlikely"* to be asked. Awareness only.

### 3. The four congruence criteria — and the AAA trap

**SSS / SAS / ASA / RHS.** Three pieces of information, but **NOT three angles**. Paul at geometry-1-13 [02:17]: *"if you prove that two triangles have all equal angles … AAA does not count. … similar triangles could have completely different length sides."* AAA gives similarity only, never congruence.

### 4. The three enlargement-scaling rules

```
k   = image length / object length    (1-D)
k²  = image area   / object area      (2-D)
k³  = image volume / object volume    (3-D)
```

The exponent matches the dimensionality. `k = 2` ⇒ 4× area, 8× volume. Fractional `k` is still an enlargement (it shrinks). Paul at geometry-1-19 [07:01]: *"the enlargements is probably the most important [transformation] on the course."*

## 🚨 LOAD-BEARING — the five things that win or lose the geometry question

### 1. The three LC proofs — reproduce the exact prescribed structure

These are the only proofs you should expect to be examined. The marking scheme grades the **structure**, not the cleverness. Use Paul's five-heading template every time: **Diagram → Given → To Prove → Construction → Proof.**

| Proof | Tested years | Where the structural marks live |
|---|---|---|
| Theorem 11 | 2024 P2 | The TWO auxiliary parallel lines (Construction 5 twice) + ASA congruence on △ABE' ≅ △F'B'E' |
| Theorem 12 | 2018, 2025 DF | Divide AX/XB into s/t equal parts; the family of parallels; apply T11 |
| Theorem 13 | 2021 | Construct AXY on the larger triangle; SAS congruence with DEF; XY∥BC via corresponding angles; apply T12 |

> 🚨 **The MS scores deviations harshly.** Paul at geometry-1-1 [01:09]: *"On the leave insert higher level course you need to be able to derive the proof of theorem 11, theorem 12 and theorem 13."* Skipping the Construction line, replacing algebra with prose, or omitting the justification on any equality all lose marks. Reproduce verbatim. (Same tactical advice applies on Paper 1 prescribed proofs.)

### 2. Similar triangles — Paul's universal five-step workflow

This is the single most-applied technique on Paper 2. Tested **every year**. From geometry-1-16:

1. **Prove similar** — match TWO angle pairs, justify each (common / vertically opposite / alternate / corresponding / angle-in-semicircle / both 90°).
2. **Redraw** the triangles separately if they share vertices or overlap.
3. **Write all THREE proportional fractions** as a chain of equals.
4. **Discard** the fraction with too many unknowns.
5. **Substitute + cross-multiply** to solve.

> 🚨 Paul at geometry-1-16 [04:42]: *"whenever we have similar triangles I want you to be able to try and produce those three fractions then you're going to sub into two of the fractions to find an unknown."* **Internalise this five-step pattern — it answers nearly every Paper 2 similar-triangles question.**
>
> 🚨 **Every angle equality MUST be justified on the same line.** Paul at [06:06]: *"every time we do it we have to justify it with either an axiom, a theorem or a corollary. You will not get the marks if you make a statement without justifying it."* Tested every year 2015–2025.

### 3. Congruent triangles — two-column structure + justified equalities

Four criteria (SSS / SAS / ASA / RHS). For each exam-question proof:

- Left column = first triangle, right column = second triangle, one matched pair per row, justification on the right.
- Three pairs of equalities → name the criterion → conclude congruent.
- Common justifications: *given*, *common side*, *opposite sides in a parallelogram* (T9), *alternate / corresponding* (T5), *vertically opposite* (T1), *isosceles base angles* (T2), *both 90°*, *angle in semicircle* (Corollary 3).

> 🚨 **AAA is NOT congruence — it's only similarity.** This is the most-tested trap (geometry-1-13 [02:17–02:36]). And **SSA is the ambiguous case** — two different triangles can share it. The included angle for SAS must lie BETWEEN the two known sides.
>
> Tested 2022 DF, 2023, 2023 DF, 2025 — every recent year.

### 4. Circle questions — identify the arc, then mechanically apply Theorem 19 + corollaries

Every circle-theorem question turns on **arc identification**. Paul at geometry-1-11-questions [01:38]: *"if I follow one of the arms I get to this point, if I follow the other arm I get to this point. So the arc in which this angle is standing on is this arc."* The two arms terminate at the two endpoints of the arc — misidentifying the arc is the #1 student error.

| Tool | When |
|---|---|
| **T19** (central = 2 × inscribed) | Two angles on the same arc, one at centre + one at edge |
| **Corollary 2** (angles on same arc equal) | Two inscribed angles standing on the same arc |
| **Corollary 3** (angle in semicircle = 90°) | One side of the angle is a diameter — free 90° |
| **Corollary 5** (cyclic-quad opposite angles sum to 180°) | Four points on a circle |
| **T20** (tangent ⊥ radius) | Tangent meets the radius at point of contact — free 90° |

> 🚨 **Composite angles at a vertex sum to the whole.** When two angles are labelled at a single vertex, the interior angle of the surrounding shape is their SUM. Don't treat them as isolated. (geometry-1-11-questions content_warning #3.)
>
> Tested 2019, 2022, 2024 DF.

### 5. Enlargements — `k`, `k²`, `k³` and the centre-of-enlargement technique

Paul at geometry-1-19 [07:01]: *"the enlargements is probably the most important [transformation] on the course."* The five examinable facts:

- `k = |XP'| / |XP|` for any corresponding pair (X = centre).
- Area scales by `k²`, volume by `k³`.
- The centre, object point, and image point are **collinear** — extend corresponding-point lines until they meet to find the centre.
- An enlargement is a similarity transformation — Theorem 13 applies to the image.
- Fractional `k` shrinks (still called an "enlargement").

> 🚨 Tested 2022 DF, 2025 P2 Q-multi (two sub-parts), 2025 DF. Rising weight — expect at least one enlargement part in 2026.

## 🎯 The 7 techniques you must execute without thinking

1. **Five-heading proof template** — write `Diagram | Given | To Prove | Construction | Proof` at the top of every prescribed-proof answer. Auto-mark structure even before you've solved.
2. **Three-colour (or symbol) tagging** for corresponding sides — for each equal-angle pair, mark with a colour; for each side, mark with the colour of the angle it's opposite. Same-colour sides go in the same fraction. (geometry-1-10, geometry-1-16.)
3. **Redraw overlapping triangles separately** — when one triangle sits inside another, extract each to its own diagram before writing the fractions.
4. **The semicircle "bridge through a third triangle" pattern** — when △X and △Y can't be directly proven similar, prove each is similar to a third triangle Z; transitivity closes it. Paul flags this at geometry-1-16 [25:24]: *"the most common kind of question."*
5. **Arc-identification via tracing the arms** — first action on any circle question.
6. **Centre-of-enlargement construction** — draw lines through ≥2 (object, image) pairs; they meet at the centre.
7. **Mark every 90° free angle** — tangent ⊥ radius, diameter-subtended-angle, perpendicular-from-right-angle-vertex. Annotate immediately on the diagram.

## ⚠ Common traps — where students lose marks

| Trap | Fix |
|---|---|
| Stating angle/side equalities without justification | Every line: equality on the left, reason on the right |
| Treating AAA as congruence | AAA gives similarity only. Need 3 pieces incl. ≥1 side |
| SSA (non-included angle) treated as SAS | Included angle MUST lie between the two sides |
| Misidentifying which arc an inscribed angle stands on | Trace BOTH arms to the circle — those points are the arc's endpoints |
| Treating composite angles at a vertex as isolated | Sum them — the cyclic-quad-180° rule applies to the FULL interior angle |
| Forgetting to mark 90° at tangent–radius contact | Draw the right-angle box on the diagram before writing equations |
| Using a fraction containing the auxiliary construction line | Discard fractions containing construction-only segments (Paul: *"I do not want to have an AD at all."*) |
| Reproducing a proof in prose without the Construction line | The MS awards explicit method marks for the Construction step |
| `k` confusion: applying `k` to an area instead of `k²` | Match exponent to dimensionality: 1-D → k, 2-D → k², 3-D → k³ |
| Proving similar by matching only ONE angle pair | Need TWO matched pairs (third follows by Theorem 4) |

## 📋 Question-type triage — reading the question wording

| Phrase | Strategy |
|---|---|
| *"Prove Theorem 11/12/13"* | Five-heading template, full Construction line, exact prescribed structure |
| *"Show that △ABC is similar to △XYZ"* | Two-angle-match proof, justify each angle |
| *"Hence find \|XY\|"* | Use the similarity from the previous part — write three fractions, discard one, cross-multiply |
| *"Prove △ABC ≅ △XYZ"* | Two-column structure, three matched pairs, name SSS/SAS/ASA/RHS |
| *"Find the scale factor"* | `k = image length / object length`; pick any corresponding-side pair |
| *"Find the centre of enlargement"* | Extend ≥2 corresponding-point lines until they meet |
| *"Construct the centroid / circumcentre / orthocentre"* | Construction 21 / 16 / 22 — show the auxiliary lines (medians / perpendicular bisectors / altitudes) |
| *"Give an example of a statement whose converse is not true"* | *"All rectangles are parallelograms"* — converse false |

## 💡 Three exam-day tips that move the needle

1. **For any prescribed proof, write the five headings BEFORE solving.** `Diagram | Given | To Prove | Construction | Proof` at the top of the page. Structure marks are awarded before content marks. This is the cheapest 2 marks on Paper 2.

2. **For every similar-triangles question, write all three fractions in a chain of equals.** Even before you know which two you'll use. Setting up `|AB|/|XY| = |AC|/|XZ| = |BC|/|YZ|` on the page wins the proportional-sides method mark whether or not you arrive at the right number.

3. **Mark free 90° angles on the diagram the instant you see a tangent, a diameter, or a perpendicular-drop.** These are the unlocks that turn "apparently insufficient information" into "enough to solve." Don't wait until you need them.

## 🔗 Cross-strand connections (where else Geometry fires)

- **Geometry ↔ Trigonometry 1** — Pythagoras (Theorem 14) is geometry's bequest to trig; every right-triangle problem leans on it. The Pythagoras proof itself (geometry-1-10) IS the canonical similar-triangles exercise.
- **Geometry ↔ The Line** — Constructions 16, 21, 22 (circumcentre, centroid, orthocentre) are taught jointly with The Line; the section formula (2:1) is what locates the centroid in coordinate geometry.
- **Geometry ↔ The Circle** — Theorem 20 (tangent ⊥ radius) + Theorem 19 corollaries are the spine of every circle-equation tangency question.
- **Geometry ↔ AVM (cone/sphere)** — Similar triangles is the standard technique for cone-rate problems (2024 P2 had a cone with similar-triangles in the recipe).
- **Geometry ↔ Transformation geometry** — Enlargement IS a similarity transformation; image triangle is similar by Theorem 13.

> Similar triangles is not "one of the techniques" on Paper 2. It is the spine. Even when the question is labelled Coordinate Geometry, Trigonometry, or Volumes, the middle 30 seconds of the working are almost always a similar-triangles ratio.
