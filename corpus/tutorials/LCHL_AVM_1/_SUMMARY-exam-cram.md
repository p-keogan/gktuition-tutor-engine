## 🧭 Why AVM deserves the first 90 minutes

From the 11-year exam-trends analysis (2015–2025):

- **AVM is technically a Paper 2 strand** — 13 P2 parts across 6/11 years (2017, 2019, 2020, 2021, 2023, 2025). Average ≈ 1.2 parts per P2 paper, but heavily concentrated when it does appear (5 parts in 2020 alone).
- **The bigger story is cross-strand.** AVM formulas are the *geometric scaffold* of Paper 1 rate problems and Paper 2 optimisation Section B questions. Six of the 11 years used AVM material inside Differentiation 20 rate problems on P1.
- **2024–25 surge — entirely cross-strand.** 2024 P1 Q9 (hemisphere `V = (π/12)x³` + related rates), 2024 DF P1 Q9 (cone candle + related rates), 2024 P2 Q8 (cylinder-in-sphere optimisation), 2025 P2 Q7 (submarine — hemisphere + cylinder + cone in one question), 2025 DF P2 Q8 (football sphere). The strand itself sits at average frequency; the *formulas* are exploding in use.
- **Section A vs B:** AVM is almost entirely Section B — it appears as the geometric setup of a 40-mark calculus or optimisation question. Standalone Section A AVM is rare.

**The takeaway.** AVM 1.1 (Paul's [00:08] anchor: *"it pops up in differentiation, it pops up in integration, it pops up in different parts of the course"*) is the formula toolkit for every shape calculation in the LCHL course. Marks lost here cascade through Section B Q7/Q8/Q9/Q10 of every recent paper.

## 📚 Learning work — what must be in your head before you sit down

These are the items the log tables **don't** give you. Drill until they're automatic.

### 1. The cone has THREE dimensions related by Pythagoras

```
R² + h² = L²       (R = radius, h = perpendicular height, L = slant height)
```

> Know any two, find the third. Volume uses `h`; curved surface area uses `L`. Read the formula carefully — the variable in the formula tells you which height. (AVM 1.1 [15:19].)

### 2. `1 litre = 1000 cm³` — the one conversion to memorise

Paul at `avm-1-2` [00:59]: *"the one that's really really important here ... one litre is equivalent to 1,000 centimetres cubed."* Every real-world tank / funnel / container question bridges between length-cubed and capacity via this. The 2024 P1 Q9(c) mark-scheme notes "states 3 litres = 3000 cm³" as a baseline mark of merit — skipping the conversion is a 1000× scale error.

### 3. The unit-square / unit-cube derivation (don't memorise — derive)

```
1 m  → 100 cm           (linear factor)
1 m² → 100² = 10,000 cm²
1 m³ → 100³ = 1,000,000 cm³
```

> Paul at `avm-1-2` [03:40]: *"I don't expect you to learn this off but if you're in the exam and you need to convert from one to the other just draw yourself a square and label it accordingly."* Same principle as the `k², k³` enlargement rules from Geometry 1.19.

### 4. Hemisphere volume — half a sphere

```
V_hemisphere = ⁴⁄₆ πR³ = ²⁄₃ πR³
```

> Not on page 10. The full sphere is. Halve it. Tested 2018 P2 (sphere + bar railing), 2024 P1 Q9 (hemisphere as a function of `x`), 2025 P2 Q7 (hemisphere + cylinder + cone submarine).

## 🚨 LOAD-BEARING — the five things that win or lose the AVM question

### 1. The cone's three-dimension Pythagoras

> The cone has **three** dimensions: radius `R`, perpendicular height `h`, slant height `L`. They form a right triangle with `R² + h² = L²`.

| Given | Find | Method |
|---|---|---|
| `R` and `h` | `L` | Pythagoras: `L = √(R² + h²)` |
| `R` and `L` | `h` | Pythagoras: `h = √(L² − R²)` |
| `CSA` and `L` | `R`, then `h` | `πRL = CSA` → solve for `R`; then Pythagoras for `h` |

> 🚨 **Volume uses `h`, curved SA uses `L`.** Paul at `avm-1-1` [15:25]: *"There's a radius, a height and a slant height."* Mixing these is the #1 cone mark-loss. **The exam will give you two of the three and ask for a quantity that depends on the third.** Tested 2017, 2020, 2022 DF P1, 2024 DF P1, 2025 P2.

### 2. The consistent-cross-section rule

> **Volume = (area of base) × (height)** for any shape with a cross-section that is identical the whole way through: cylinder, cube, rectangular block, triangular prism. This rule does **NOT** apply to the cone, pyramid, or sphere — the cross-section shrinks.

This is what lets you compute the volume of a shape that isn't on the log tables (Paul's "triangular trough" example). It is also what *fails* to apply to cones, and is the reason the cone formula has a `⅓` factor.

> 🚨 Paul at `avm-1-1` [11:04]: *"The volume of a shape can be the base multiplied by the height ... as long as the base here is a circle and that circle exists the whole way along the cylinder up to the top."* Tested 2019, 2023, 2025 P2.

### 3. `1 litre = 1000 cm³` — capacity-to-volume bridge

Almost any real-world AVM question involves a tank, container, funnel, or hemisphere being filled or drained. The dimensions are in cm or m; the answer is asked for in litres.

> 🚨 Tested 2024 P1 Q9(c) (the 1000× scale-error trap), 2024 DF P2 (km/h ← mm/s conversion), 2025 P2 (scale conversion via percentage error).

### 4. Curved SA vs Total SA — read the wording

| Shape | Curved SA only | Total SA |
|---|---|---|
| Cylinder | `2πRh` | `2πRh + 2πR²` |
| Cone | `πRL` | `πRL + πR²` |
| Sphere | `4πR²` | `4πR²` (one continuous surface) |

> 🚨 *"Curved"* means the wraparound only. *"Total"* adds the flat ends. The two answers differ by `2πR²` (cylinder) or `πR²` (cone) — a quick read of the wording is the entire mark. AVM 1.1 [10:00].

### 5. Units in the final answer — every time

> 🚨 Paul at `avm-1-1` [22:55]: *"always, always remember in this topic you need units."*

Lengths in cm, areas in cm², volumes in cm³. The SEC mark scheme deducts marks for missing units even when arithmetic is fully correct (2021 P1 Q10: *"Full Credit −1 for missing or incorrect units"*). Tested every year. Build the units check into your final-answer routine.

## 🎯 The 7 techniques you must execute without thinking

1. **Identify which formula connects what you have to what you need.** Q-skeleton from AVM 1.1: (a) what are you given? (b) what's asked? (c) which formula links them? (d) solve for the unknown dimension; (e) sub into another formula to get the asked-for quantity.
2. **Similar triangles on a cone.** Any horizontal cross-section of a cone is similar to the whole cone — so radius-to-height ratio is constant. Used to collapse two-variable cone problems to one variable (`h = kr` substitution). Tested 2017 P2, 2020 P2, 2022 DF P1, 2023 DF P1, 2024 P2 — every cone optimisation.
3. **Algebraic substitution when a constraint links two dimensions.** "Height equals diameter" ⇒ `h = 2r` ⇒ `V = ⅓πr²(2r) = ⅔πr³`. (2022 DF P1 Q10, 2024 DF P1 Q9.) The cone is now a one-variable function — differentiate it.
4. **Implicit differentiation of a shape formula** (cross-strand into Differentiation 20). Sphere: `V = ⁴⁄₃πr³` ⇒ `dV/dt = 4πr² · (dr/dt)`. Cone: `V = ⅓πr²h` with `h = kr` ⇒ `V = (k/3)πr³` ⇒ `dV/dt = kπr² · (dr/dt)`. Tested 2016, 2017, 2018, 2020, 2021, 2024 P1, 2024 DF P1.
5. **Trapezoidal rule formula application.** `h` is the **strip width** (not a height); `y₁, …, yₙ` are the **strip-boundary heights**. First and last get coefficient 1; every interior height gets coefficient 2. Paul at `avm-1-3` [00:34]: *"h is the width of each section… the y's represent the heights while h represents the width."*
6. **Pair the trapezoidal estimate with an integration check.** Paul at `avm-1-3` [03:06]: *"the trapezoidal rule is likely to come up in conjunction with integration."* The canonical 2015 P1 Q3 pattern: estimate via trapezoidal, find exact via integration, compute percentage error.
7. **Unit conversion via squaring/cubing the linear factor.** Linear factor `k` ⇒ area factor `k²`, volume factor `k³`. Same rule as enlargements (Geometry 1.19). Derive on the page — don't gamble on remembering the count of zeros.

## ⚠ Common traps — where students lose marks

| Trap | Fix |
|---|---|
| Cone volume using slant height `L` instead of perpendicular height `h` | Volume uses `h`; curved SA uses `L`. Read the formula. |
| Forgetting the `⅓` for cone (writing `πR²h` instead of `⅓πR²h`) | Consistent-cross-section rule does NOT apply to cones — the `⅓` is what makes it not a cylinder |
| Skipping the `1 L = 1000 cm³` conversion | A 1000× scale error. 2024 P1 Q9(c) explicit MS note. |
| Sphere "sitting on top of a bar": subtracting radius instead of diameter | The sphere has vertical extent `2r`. (2018 P2 Q7.) |
| Frustum vs full cone | A frustum is a cone with the apex cut off. Volume = (large cone) − (small cone). Use similar triangles to find the small-cone height. |
| Missing units on the final answer | SEC deducts marks every year. Build the units check into your last line. |
| `1 m² = 100 cm²` (wrong — forgot to square) | `(100)² = 10,000 cm²`. Draw the unit square if you doubt it. |
| `1 m³ = 1000 cm³` (wrong — confused with `1 L = 1000 cm³`) | `(100)³ = 1,000,000 cm³`. Litres are not the same as cubic metres. |
| Treating a cone partially filled with water as a cylinder | The water forms a smaller similar cone — use similar triangles on the radius and height. |
| Using the cone formula `⅓πR²h` when `h` ≠ given height | If the question gives slant height `L`, find `h` via Pythagoras *before* substituting |
| Trapezoidal rule: `h` confused with a y-value | `h` is the strip *width*, not a height. The y-values are the ordinate heights. |
| Curve below the x-axis in a trapezoidal question | Use absolute values of the y-coordinates — area is always positive |
| Answer "in terms of π" but π evaluated numerically | Leave π symbolic — don't multiply it out. (2021 P2 Q5(a)(ii).) |

## 📋 Question-type triage — reading the question wording

| Phrase | Strategy |
|---|---|
| *"In terms of π"* | Leave π symbolic — don't compute `π ≈ 3.14...` |
| *"Show that …"* | State every formula you use; sub the given values; method marks are heavy |
| *"To 1 decimal place"* / *"correct to the nearest cm"* | Compute at full precision, round only at the end |
| *"Net of the cylinder/cone"* | The curved surface unrolls into a rectangle (cylinder: `2πR × h`) or sector (cone: arc length `2πR`, radius `L`) |
| *"Capacity in litres"* with cm dimensions | Compute volume in cm³, then divide by 1000 |
| *"Fits exactly inside …"* | The inner shape is inscribed — set up the inscribed-shape constraint (e.g. cylinder's height + diameter via sphere's diameter) |
| *"Two solids have equal volume"* / *"recast / melted"* | Equate the two volume formulas; solve for the unknown dimension |
| *"At what rate is …"* / *"how fast is …"* | This is Differentiation 20 territory — implicit-differentiate the shape formula with respect to `t` |
| *"Maximum / minimum volume"* | Use a constraint to reduce to one variable, then differentiate (Differentiation 15 territory) |
| *"Slant height"* mentioned | Cone or pyramid. Pythagoras between `R`, `h`, `L`. |

## 💡 Three exam-day tips that move the needle

1. **Sketch first, label every dimension you're given, then choose the formula.** AVM questions where students go wrong almost always have a sketch issue underneath — the student tried to substitute into a formula without first identifying which dimension they had. A 30-second labelled sketch saves the question.

2. **Use the log tables for the formulas, working memory for the dimensions.** Page 10 has every 3D volume and surface-area formula you need. The job in front of you is choosing *which* formula and *which* dimension is which — not recall.

3. **Show units on every intermediate line.** If `R = 5 cm`, write `R = 5 cm`, not `R = 5`. If the question gives `cm` and asks for `litres`, do the conversion *as a labelled step*, not silently at the end. The SEC mark scheme rewards visible unit-handling and punishes its absence.

## 🔗 Cross-strand connections (where else AVM fires)

- **AVM ↔ Differentiation (rate problems, §20)** — every cone/sphere/cylinder rate problem on Paper 1 starts with an AVM formula and implicit-differentiates it. Tested 2016, 2017, 2018, 2020, 2021, 2024 P1, 2024 DF P1.
- **AVM ↔ Differentiation (max/min, §15)** — cone-inscribed-in-sphere and cylinder-inscribed-in-sphere optimisation. 2024 P2 Q8 explicitly.
- **AVM ↔ Integration (§12/13)** — `avm-1-6` and `avm-1-7` are wrappers for Integration 12/13. Area between curve and x-axis (or y-axis) is canonically integration but is listed under AVM on the syllabus.
- **AVM ↔ Trigonometry (§1.1, §1.7)** — Pythagoras on the cone's right triangle (the slant-radius-height triangle); `½ab sin C` for non-right-angled triangle area.
- **AVM ↔ Geometry 1.16 (similar triangles)** — the cross-section of a cone at any height is similar to the cone itself. The radius scales with height by the same ratio. This is the load-bearing trick for every partial-fill cone question.
- **AVM ↔ The Line (§4)** — area of triangle from three vertex coordinates (page 18).
- **AVM ↔ Algebra** — the substitution discipline. When a constraint pins two cone dimensions together (`h = 2r`, `h = kr`), the cone volume collapses to a single-variable function ready for differentiation.

> AVM is the geometric scaffold for Section B Q7/Q8/Q9/Q10 on every recent paper. The 2024–25 surge is not the strand getting bigger; it's the formulas being asked for inside a calculus question.
