## 🧭 Why Trigonometry 1 deserves the first 90 minutes

From the 11-year exam-trends analysis (2015–2025, 30 papers, 1,212 tagged question-parts):

- **Trig 1 appears in 11/11 years on Paper 2.** Average ≈ 6.2 parts per paper. Total: **68 P2 parts** — the third-largest P2 strand after Probability (78) and Statistics (71).
- **Sustained recent weight.** 2023 = 7 parts, 2024 = 7, 2025 = 8 — all above the 6.2 average. The 2019–2020 papers were the previous peak (8, 8). No "surge" year, but no light year either: the strand reliably delivers ~7–8 parts in recent papers.
- **Two halves, evenly tested.** Right-angled work (Pythagoras + SOHCAHTOA — `trig-1-1` and `trig-1-2`) has **38 citations combined**; non-right-angled work (sine rule, cosine rule, area — `trig-1-5`, `trig-1-6`, `trig-1-7`) has **32 citations combined**. Both halves are mandatory. Sectors (`trig-1-8`) add 7 more.
- **Cross-strand reach inside Paper 2.** Trig 1 fires inside Geometry 1 (similar triangles + Pythagoras inside circle theorems), The Circle (radius-tangent perpendicular requires Pythagoras), The Line (the distance formula on page 18 *is* Pythagoras), and AVM (cone `R² + h² = L²`, sphere cross-sections). Differentiation rate problems on P1 frequently use a Pythagoras-set-up to relate the changing variables.

**The takeaway.** Trig 1 is the single most reliable Section A strand on Paper 2 outside Probability and Statistics — almost every paper has a right-angled question (Section A) plus a non-right-angled / sector question (Section A or B Q5/Q9). The formulas all live on page 16 plus page 9 — the marks are for **picking the right one**.

## 📚 Learning work — what must be in your head before you sit down

These are the items the log tables **don't** give you — or do give but are easy to mis-apply.

### 1. The capital-angle / small-side labelling convention

Capital `A`, `B`, `C` are the three angles; small `a`, `b`, `c` are the three sides. **Side `a` is opposite angle `A`**; side `b` opposite `B`; side `c` opposite `C`. This convention is implicit on page 16 (printed once on the right-angled-triangle diagram) and is used by all three non-right-angled formulas.

> Paul (sine-rule [02:00]): *"sides and opposite angles are in cahoots here. There's a relationship between B and B, A and A, C and C. You can't just randomly assign them, you have to always take a side on its opposite angle."* Mis-pairing side `a` with angle `B` is the canonical sine-rule setup error.

### 2. The which-rule-to-use diagnostic

| What you have | What you use |
|---|---|
| A right angle | SOHCAHTOA (1.2) + Pythagoras (1.1). **Do NOT reach for sine/cosine rules** — they work, but they're slower |
| A side and its **opposite** angle (plus one other piece) | **Sine rule** (1.5) |
| 2 sides + the **included** angle (SAS), wanting the third side | **Cosine rule** (1.6) — Scenario 1 |
| 3 sides (SSS), wanting any angle | **Cosine rule rearranged** (1.6) — Scenario 2 |
| 2 sides + included angle, wanting the area | **`½ ab sin C`** (1.7) — same SAS configuration as cosine rule Scenario 1 |

> Paul (cosine-rule [01:50]): *"I know this side but I don't know its opposite angle … so I cannot use the sine rule."* That's the **negative test** for sine rule and the **positive trigger** for cosine rule. Every non-right-angled question fits exactly one of these rows.

### 3. Three Pythagorean triples worth knowing on sight

`(3, 4, 5)`, `(5, 12, 13)`, `(8, 15, 17)`. Multiples count too — `(6, 8, 10) = 2·(3, 4, 5)`. Recognising these saves 30 seconds and acts as a sanity check on Pythagoras arithmetic.

### 4. Reject-negative-length rule when Pythagoras produces a quadratic

When sides are algebraic (`x`, `2x+6`, etc.) and Pythagoras yields a quadratic with two roots, **test each root in the original side expressions and reject any that produces a negative length**.

> Paul (Pythagoras [07:35]): *"we clearly cannot have a negative length here so in this case x equals minus 5 over 3 was an invalid answer ... my final answer is x equals 3."*

### 5. Sketch-required-for-marks rule

If the question says *"in surd form"*, *"a/b where a, b ∈ ℤ"*, *"without using your calculator"*, or *"sketch a right-angled triangle to find …"*, you **must** sketch the triangle and use Pythagoras to recover the missing side. Calculator-only solutions give the right number and lose the method marks.

> Paul (sketching [00:37]): *"it's easy to get the answer but it's not necessarily easy to get the marks … you wouldn't get the marks."*

## 🚨 LOAD-BEARING — the five things that win or lose the Trig 1 question

### 1. "Right-angled" first — SOHCAHTOA + Pythagoras, not the rules

Before reaching for the sine or cosine rule, **read the question / picture for a right angle**. If there is one, SOHCAHTOA + Pythagoras is the faster, cleaner route.

> 🚨 Paul (sine rule [00:25]): *"You can apply these rules to right angle triangles but you shouldn't because it'll probably be, it just makes the question much harder."* Tested every year (Pythagoras + SOHCAHTOA together = 38 P2 citations across 11 years).

### 2. The cosine rule has two distinct setups — and one rearrangement to memorise

| Scenario | Given | Rearrangement |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 2 sides + the **included** angle → find the third side | Use as printed: `a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A` |
| 2 | 3 sides → find any angle | **Rearrange first** to `cos A = (b² + c² − a²)/(2bc)`, then substitute |

> 🚨 Paul (cosine rule [06:24]): *"in this scenario, my advice to you is to manipulate the cosine rule before you sub in. It's going to be incredibly easy now. When I sub in, I'll have cos A straight away."* Substituting first then rearranging is equivalent but introduces sign-drop risk. Tested 2015, 2016, 2017, 2018, 2019, 2021, 2022, 2022 DF, 2023, 2024, 2024 DF, 2025, 2025 DF — **every paper**.

A negative `cos A` from a calculator is **correct** — it just means the angle is obtuse. Don't "correct" it.

### 3. `½ ab sin C` — the `C` is the angle, not a side

The included-angle area formula requires **two sides and the angle BETWEEN them**. The `C` in `sin C` is an angle. The side `c` (opposite `C`) does **not** appear in the formula.

> 🚨 Paul (area [00:45]): *"The C there, that's the capital C, obviously refers to the angle that you know. Which means that if you're dealing with the angle C, the opposite side would be the side C. And thus, the small C is NOT in the formula."*

Always label area answers in **squared units** (m², cm², units²) — Paul flags this twice in the video. Tested 2016, 2019, 2021, 2022, 2022 DF, 2023, 2024, 2024 DF, 2025 P2 — every recent year.

### 4. Sectors — pick the row by checking θ's units, not by habit

Page 9 carries **four** formulas: arc length in degrees, arc length in radians, sector area in degrees, sector area in radians. The choice is determined entirely by the units of θ in the question.

| θ in degrees | θ in radians |
|---|---|
| `L = 2πR · θ/360` | `L = Rθ` (no π) |
| `A = πR² · θ/360` | `A = ½R²θ` (no π) |

> 🚨 Paul (sector [01:38]): *"If the angle is in degrees then we'll use the formula in the maths tables for degrees. If the angle is in radians we'll use the formula for radians."* The radian forms contain **no π** — the π is already absorbed into the radian value. Students who memorised the degree form often insert a stray π in the radian setup. Tested 2017, 2019, 2020, 2022, 2023 DF, 2024, 2024 DF, 2025.

### 5. Sine rule — the ambiguous case (SSA)

When given **two sides and a non-included angle (SSA)**, both `θ` and `180° − θ` can be valid answers. The calculator gives the acute interpretation by default. Read the diagram — if an obtuse triangle is geometrically possible, you must consider both.

> 🚨 Tested 2017, 2019, 2021, 2023, 2024 P2 — recurring SSA configurations. The default exam pattern: question gives a side, its opposite angle, and another side; asks for the third side or its opposite angle.

## 🎯 The 7 techniques you must execute without thinking

1. **Identify the hypotenuse first.** The hypotenuse is the side opposite the right angle (and the longest side). Once labelled, Pythagoras and SOHCAHTOA both lock in correctly.
2. **SOHCAHTOA in a right triangle.** State the trig ratio in symbols, substitute, solve. To find a side: `sin θ = opp/hyp ⇒ opp = hyp · sin θ`. To find the angle: `θ = sin⁻¹(opp/hyp)`. Calculator in **DEG** mode unless told otherwise.
3. **Sine rule setup.** Use only **two** of the three fractions — pick the two where you know three of the four values. Cross-multiply, solve.
4. **Cosine rule — Scenario 1 (find a side).** Substitute directly into `a² = b² + c² − 2bc cos A` with `A` as the included angle, take square root, reject negative.
5. **Cosine rule — Scenario 2 (find an angle).** Rearrange to `cos A = (b² + c² − a²)/(2bc)` FIRST, then substitute, then `A = cos⁻¹(...)`.
6. **Sector formula selection.** Look at θ's units. Degrees → top row of page 9. Radians → bottom row.
7. **Sketch + Pythagoras for surd-form ratios.** Draw the right triangle, label the two ratio sides, use `a² + b² = c²` to find the third, then read off any requested ratio. Leave surds un-decimalised.

## ⚠ Common traps — where students lose marks

| Trap | Fix |
|---|---|
| Reaching for sine/cosine rule on a right-angled triangle | Use SOHCAHTOA + Pythagoras — faster and cleaner |
| Mis-pairing side `a` with angle `B` in the sine rule | Capital A, B, C are angles; small a, b, c are the opposite sides. Always |
| Setting up sine rule with **three** fractions (two unknowns) | Use only two fractions per equation — one unknown |
| Cosine-rule Scenario 2: substituting before rearranging | Rearrange to `cos A = (b² + c² − a²)/(2bc)` **first** |
| `½ ab sin C` with the side `c` plugged in instead of the angle | `C` is the **angle** between sides `a` and `b`. The side `c` doesn't appear |
| Forgetting to square the units on an area answer | Always m², cm², units² — Paul flags it explicitly |
| Sector: using the degree formula when θ is in radians (or vice versa) | Check θ's units before picking the row of page 9 |
| Sector: inserting π in the radian formula | Radian forms (`Rθ`, `½R²θ`) have **no π** — already absorbed |
| Sine rule SSA: missing the obtuse interpretation `180° − θ` | Sketch the triangle. If obtuse is possible, both answers are valid |
| Pythagoras with algebraic sides: keeping a negative-length root | Test each root in the side expressions; reject any that gives a negative length |
| `(2x + 6)² = 4x² + 36` (dropping the cross term) | Double-bracket expansion: `(2x + 6)² = 4x² + 24x + 36` |
| Decimalising a surd-form answer (`√149 ≈ 12.21`) | Leave the surd. If the radicand has no square factor, write it as-is |
| Calculator in radian mode for a degree question (or vice versa) | Check the mode at the start; trig ratios silently wrong otherwise |

## 📋 Question-type triage — reading the question wording

| Phrase | Strategy |
|---|---|
| *"Right-angled triangle"* (named or shown) | SOHCAHTOA + Pythagoras — **not** the rules |
| *"Find the length of the third side"* + one side + opposite angle | Sine rule |
| *"Find the length of [side]"* + 2 sides + included angle | Cosine rule, Scenario 1 |
| *"Find the size of angle [X]"* + 3 sides | Cosine rule, Scenario 2 — rearrange first |
| *"Find the area of [the triangle]"* + 2 sides + included angle | `½ ab sin C` |
| *"Find the area"* + question gives 3 sides only | Cosine rule (find any angle) → then `½ ab sin C` — two-step |
| *"In surd form"* / *"a, b ∈ ℤ"* / *"without your calculator"* | Sketch the right triangle, use Pythagoras, leave surds |
| *"Arc length / sector area"* + θ in radians | Page 9 — radian formulas (`Rθ`, `½R²θ`). No π |
| *"Arc length / sector area"* + θ in degrees | Page 9 — degree formulas (with `θ/360` and π baked in) |
| *"Two possible values"* / *"both answers"* for an angle | Sine rule ambiguous case — calculator gives one, `180° − θ` gives the other |
| *"Angle of elevation / depression"* | Right-angled triangle, SOHCAHTOA. Elevation = opposite/adjacent typically |
| *"Show that the triangle is right-angled"* | Verify Pythagoras: longest side² = sum of other two sides² |

## 💡 Three exam-day tips that move the needle

1. **State which rule and why, before substituting.** One line: "Using the cosine rule (two sides + included angle)…". This is a method mark every time and forces you to confirm the rule fits the configuration. Mis-applied rules lose method marks even when the arithmetic is right.

2. **Delay the calculator until the final line.** Paul's discipline at sine-rule [07:53]: *"I often advise my students not to use their calculator until this line because at least at this stage you haven't rounded off at all and our answer will be as accurate as possible."* Leave `(6 · sin 100°) / 17` symbolic until the last step. Cuts compounding rounding error.

3. **Check calculator mode at the start of every trig question.** DEG for degrees, RAD for radians. The calculator gives wrong values silently — there's no error message. Five seconds at the top of the question saves a question's worth of marks.

## 🔗 Cross-strand connections (where else Trig 1 fires)

- **Trig 1 ↔ Geometry 1** — similar-triangles questions almost always finish with Pythagoras or SOHCAHTOA on the smaller triangle (Theorem 11 chord-perpendicular constructions).
- **Trig 1 ↔ The Circle** — the radius-tangent perpendicular (Theorem 19) creates right triangles whose missing side is found via Pythagoras (tested 2015, 2017, 2020, 2021, 2024 DF).
- **Trig 1 ↔ The Line** — the page-18 distance formula `d = √((x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²)` **is** Pythagoras applied to coordinate separations.
- **Trig 1 ↔ AVM 1** — cone geometry has `R² + h² = L²` (radius, height, slant — Pythagoras on the cross-section). Sphere cross-sections produce right triangles relating chord length, radius, and centre-distance.
- **Trig 1 ↔ Trig 4 (compound angle / double angle)** — 2023 P2 proof of `sin(A+B)` from `cos(A−B)` uses the complementary-angle identity `sin A = cos(90° − A)` from `trig-1-4`. The double-angle `sin 2W = 2 sin W cos W` is often paired with right-triangle SOHCAHTOA when one ratio is given.
- **Trig 1 ↔ Differentiation (rate problems, P1)** — Pythagoras is the standard "relate the variables" identity in related-rates questions (ladder against wall, expanding balloon cross-section, shadow problems).
- **Trig 1 ↔ Geometry 1 / Paper 2 Proofs** — 2022 P2 prescribed proof was Theorem 17 (Pythagoras itself). Know the construction.

> Trig 1 is the silent partner of half the Paper 2 paper. The page-16 trio plus the page-9 sector formulas are referenced by almost every geometric question on the paper.
