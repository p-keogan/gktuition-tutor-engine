## 🧭 Why The Line deserves the first 90 minutes

From the 11-year exam-trends analysis (2015–2025, 30 papers, 1,212 tagged question-parts):

- **The Line appears in 11/11 years on Paper 2.** Total: **46 P2-main parts**, average ≈ 4.2 per paper. Section A's bread and butter.
- **Recent surge:** 2024 had **6** line parts; 2025 had **6**. Prior 9-year average was ≈ 3.8. Plan as if 2026 is line-heavy too.
- **Section A leans heavier** — most line parts appear as Q1 or Q2 standalone, with the rest spread across Section B (often as the scaffolding for a Circle or Geometry question).
- **Cross-strand reach:** The Line is the *enabling toolkit* for The Circle (tangent ⟺ perp distance from centre = radius — tested almost every year), for Geometry 1 (Constructions 16, 21, 22 are jointly housed in The Line tutorials 7–9), and for Algebra (modulus-split discipline transfers identically — `|X| = k → ±X = k`).

**The takeaway.** This is the strand where **page 18 of the log tables does most of your thinking for you.** What you bring is the discipline to rearrange, read off signs, and split the modulus.

## 📚 Learning work — what must be in your head before you sit down

These items page 18 **doesn't** explicitly tell you. Drill until they're automatic.

### 1. Perpendicular slopes multiply to `−1` — flip AND negate

If a line has slope `m`, the perpendicular slope is `m_⊥ = −1/m`. Two operations: **reciprocal** AND **sign flip**. From `the-line-1`: tested every year. Vertical line is the exception — slope is **undefined**, not zero.

### 2. The point-on-line check

> A point `(x₁, y₁)` lies on a line `ax + by + c = 0` ⟺ substituting gives **exactly `0`**.

From `the-line-2`. Used directly in 2018 (Verify A on m), 2021 (Find k), 2022 DF (parameterised point), 2025 (Find p). Identical mechanic each year.

### 3. The general-form rearrangement (sign discipline)

Before substituting into the page-18 distance or angle formulas, **rearrange the line into `ax + by + c = 0` with everything on the left**. The most common slip is forgetting to flip the sign on the constant when moving it across the equals.

```
y = mx + c     →    mx − y + c = 0    (so a = m, b = −1, c = c — sign as given)
ax + by = k    →    ax + by − k = 0   (so the constant flips sign)
```

### 4. The modulus equation split — `|X| = k → ±X = k`

Used in both the distance formula and the area formula whenever there's an unknown. Two linear cases, two candidate answers. Then **check each against the diagram** — often only one is geometrically valid.

## 🚨 LOAD-BEARING — the five things that win or lose The Line question

### 1. Perpendicular distance formula — page 18, with sign discipline

> `d = |ax₁ + by₁ + c| / √(a² + b²)` where the line is in form `ax + by + c = 0`.

The 11-year recurring rule. Tested 2015, 2016, 2018, 2019, 2020, 2021, 2023, 2024, 2025, 2023 DF, 2024 DF, 2025 DF — **10 of 11 main years plus 3 DF sittings**.

> 🚨 **The numerator carries the modulus, the denominator does not.** Distance is non-negative. If you forget the modulus and your answer comes out negative, you've also probably dropped a method mark. Paul (`the-line-5`): *"The numerator carries an absolute-value `|…|`. The denominator does not — it's a square root, which is always taken as the positive root."*

### 2. Area-of-triangle: ONE vertex MUST be at the origin

> `Area = ½|x₁y₂ − x₂y₁|` where one vertex is at `(0,0)` and the other two are `(x₁,y₁)` and `(x₂,y₂)`.

> 🚨 Paul (`the-line-4` [02:02]): *"You cannot use this formula unless one of the points is on the origin."* If none of the three given vertices is at the origin, **translate the whole triangle** — apply the same `(h, k)` shift to all three vertices. The vertex you've shifted to the origin gets excluded from the substitution. Tested 2023 P2 Q3, 2024 P2 Q5, 2025 P2 Q1, 2022 DF P2.

### 3. Angle between two lines — the `±` is load-bearing

> `tan θ = ±(m₁ − m₂)/(1 + m₁m₂)` — the `±` is printed on the formula sheet.

| Scenario | What's given | What's asked | What to do with `±` |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Scenario 1** | Two slopes | The acute angle between them | Take the positive value; supplement gives obtuse |
| **Scenario 2** | One slope + the angle | The other slope (two answers) | **Keep both `+` and `−` cases** — they give the two parallel lines |

> 🚨 Paul (`the-line-6`): *"The `±` sign in front is load-bearing. It is on the formula sheet for a reason — it produces two cases that you choose between based on which scenario you're in."* Tested 2017, 2020, 2022, 2025, 2025 DF.

### 4. Tangent to a circle ⟺ perpendicular distance from centre = radius

The single highest-yield cross-strand combination. Used 2016 Q2(b), 2019 Q3(b), 2023 P2 Q4(a)(ii), 2023 DF, 2025 Q4. The Line 5 + The Circle 7 fire together.

> 🚨 **Recipe:** Extract centre `(−g, −f)` and radius `√(g² + f² − c)` from the circle equation. Use the page-18 formula with the centre as `(x₁, y₁)` and the tangent line as `ax + by + c = 0`. Set the distance equal to the radius. Square both sides if needed, solve.

### 5. Section formula — read the ratio direction

> `C` divides `[AB]` internally in ratio `m : n` (from A to B) → `C = (n·A + m·B)/(m+n)`.

> 🚨 **The "fast shortcut": `|AC| = (m/(m+n))·|AB|`.** For centroid problems (2:1 from vertex), `|AC| = (2/3)|AB|`. Used at 2017 Q3 (`P = (3G − A)/2`), 2021 Q2 (`|AD| = (2/3)|AC|`), 2022 Q2, 2024 Q5. The direction matters — `m:n` is counted **from `A`**, not from `B`.

## 🎯 The 7 techniques you must execute without thinking

1. **Rearrange any line to `ax + by + c = 0` before substituting.** Read off `a`, `b`, `c` with signs. The most common error in the strand.
2. **Slope from general form: `m = −a/b`.** Faster than rearranging to `y = mx + c` every time.
3. **Perpendicular slope: flip the reciprocal AND change the sign.** `m = 4` → `m_⊥ = −1/4`. Both operations.
4. **The "verify on line" sub-and-check** — substitute coordinates, expect `0`. Used to test, used in reverse to solve for an unknown coordinate.
5. **Translate-to-origin for the area formula** — choose the vertex closest to the origin to minimise arithmetic. Apply the **same** translation to all three vertices.
6. **Section formula in fast form**: `|AC| = (m/(m+n))·|AB|`. For ratio `2:1` from `A`, `C` is 2/3 of the way from `A` to `B`.
7. **Modulus split** — `|X| = k → ±X = k` → two linear cases → two candidate answers → geometric sanity check.

## ⚠ Common traps — where students lose marks

| Trap | Fix |
|---|---|
| Forgetting the modulus → negative distance | Distance is non-negative. Wrap `|…|` in the numerator. |
| Wrong sign on `c` after rearranging `ax + by = k` | Move everything to the left. `ax + by − k = 0` so `c = −k`. |
| Using the area formula without a vertex at origin | Translate first. All three vertices, same shift. |
| Perpendicular slope: only flipping, not negating (or vice versa) | Both. `m → −1/m`. |
| Vertical line: declaring slope `= 0` | Vertical slope is **undefined**. Horizontal slope is 0. |
| Section formula: `m:n` from B instead of A | Counted from A to B. `C = (n·A + m·B)/(m+n)`. |
| Angle question: forgetting `tan θ` returns acute by default | Supplement (180° − θ) is the obtuse. State both if asked. |
| Scenario 2 angle: collapsing `±` into one case | Two slopes is the correct answer — both are valid. |
| Closest-point-on-line via distance only | The formula gives the **length**. To find the **coordinates**, find the perpendicular through the point, intersect with the line. |
| Tangent-to-circle: setting distance equal to diameter | Tangent ⟺ perpendicular distance from centre = **radius**, not diameter. |
| Three collinear points: using slope check inconsistently | All three slopes between pairs must match, OR triangle area is 0, OR perpendicular distance is 0. Pick one and apply cleanly. |

## 📋 Question-type triage — reading the question wording

| Phrase | Strategy |
|---|---|
| *"lies on the line"* / *"contains the point"* | Substitute coordinates into `ax + by + c`, set `= 0`, solve. |
| *"perpendicular distance"* / *"shortest distance"* | Page-18 formula. Rearrange to `ax + by + c = 0` first. |
| *"find k such that the distance is …"* | Page-18 formula → modulus equation → split `±`. Two cases. |
| *"line is a tangent to the circle"* | Perpendicular distance from centre = radius. |
| *"perpendicular bisector"* | Midpoint + perpendicular slope. Two conditions = one line. |
| *"angle between the lines"* | Page-18 tan formula. Scenario 1: take the absolute value. |
| *"find the two slopes that make angle θ with …"* | Scenario 2: keep both `±` cases. |
| *"area of triangle PQR"* | If one vertex is at origin, page-18 area formula. If not, translate first. |
| *"divides `[AB]` in ratio m:n"* | Section formula. Counted from `A`. |
| *"closest point on the line to P"* | Find perpendicular through `P`, intersect with the line. Distance formula gives the length only. |
| *"centroid / circumcentre / orthocentre of triangle"* | `the-line-7/8/9` — solve a 2×2 simultaneous system on two perpendicular bisectors (circumcentre) / medians (centroid) / altitudes (orthocentre). |
| *"on which side of the line is …"* | Substitute coordinates into `ax + by + c`; sign tells you the side (`the-line-2`). |

## 💡 Three exam-day tips that move the needle

1. **Open page 18 of the log tables before you start.** Every formula in this strand is on it. Don't waste working memory memorising what's printed in front of you. Use working memory for **sign discipline** and **scenario selection**.

2. **Rearrange to `ax + by + c = 0` BEFORE you substitute.** Write `a = …, b = …, c = …` explicitly on the page, with signs. The single largest source of avoidable mark loss in this strand is misreading a sign after rearranging.

3. **Whenever an answer has a `±`, split into two cases on the page and sanity-check each against the diagram.** The modulus equation produces two algebraic answers; the geometry often eliminates one. Paul (`the-line-4` [22:58]): *"in the diagram given in the original question it was clear to see that whatever a is it's definitely positive"* — discard the extraneous solution explicitly.

## 🔗 Cross-strand connections (where else The Line fires)

- **The Line ↔ The Circle** — tangent ⟺ perp distance from centre = radius (tested most years); circle centre `(−g, −f)` plus a line constraint gives a 2×2 simultaneous system.
- **The Line ↔ Geometry 1** — Constructions 16 (circumcircle), 21 (centroid), 22 (orthocentre) are jointly housed in `the-line-7/8/9` and `geometry-1-3/4/6`. The synthetic proof lives in Geometry 1; the coordinate-geometry execution lives in The Line.
- **The Line ↔ Algebra** — the modulus split `|X| = k → ±X = k` is identical to Algebra 15. Solving the resulting simultaneous system (e.g. perp-distance + on-line) uses the substitution method from Algebra 19.
- **The Line ↔ Trigonometry 1** — `m = tan α` connects line slope to angle-with-x-axis; the cosine rule sometimes provides an alternative to the angle-between-two-lines formula.
- **The Line ↔ Statistics** — the line of best fit (regression) is a coordinate-geometry line; slope and intercept have the same meaning. Tested 2017 Q2, 2022 Q8.
- **The Line ↔ Number Theory / Probability** — occasional appearances in cross-strand parts where coordinates parameterise a sample space.

> The Line is structurally the **scaffolding strand** of Paper 2. Even when the question is labelled Circle or Geometry, the last 30 seconds of the working are almost always on page 18.
