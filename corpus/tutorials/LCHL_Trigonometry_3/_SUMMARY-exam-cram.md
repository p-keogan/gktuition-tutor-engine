## 🧭 Why Trig 3 deserves the first 90 minutes

From the 11-year exam-trends analysis (2015–2025, 30 papers, 1,212 tagged question-parts):

- **Trig 3 appears in 8/11 main years, on BOTH papers:** P1 — 7 parts (2015, 2017, 2022); P2 — 7 parts (2016, 2018, 2021, 2023, 2025). Total: **14 main-paper parts** across the period. Plus deferred sittings: 2023 DF, 2024 DF, 2025 DF.
- **The 2024 anomaly.** Main 2024 had **zero** Trig 3 — the only year in the recent run that skipped it. But the 2024 DF kept it alive (ferris-wheel question). Plan as if 2026 has at least one Trig 3 part.
- **When it appears, it dominates ~10 marks** — usually as a Section B sub-part where a real-world phenomenon (lung volume, tide, ferris wheel, machine part, temperature, daylight hours) is modelled by `y = a + b·trig(ct)`. The student is asked to read off midline, amplitude, range, period — or to recover the equation from a graph.
- **Cross-strand reach.** Trig 3 underpins **Differentiation** questions on sinusoidal models (chain rule on `cos((π/2)t)` — tested 2023 P2 Q7), **Integration** of `sin/cos` (forward direction), and **Trig 2** (radian discipline, coefficient on the angle).

**The takeaway.** Trig 3 is *one canonical template* — `y = a + b·trig(ct)` — applied across a dozen exam contexts. Internalise the template and the strand collapses to a 4-step routine.

## 📚 Learning work — what must be in your head before you sit down

These items the log tables **don't** give you. Drill until they're automatic.

### 1. The canonical general form

> ```
> y = a + b · trig(c · t)
> ```
> - `a` = **midline** (vertical shift). Range oscillates around `y = a`, not the x-axis.
> - `|b|` = **amplitude**. Sign of `b` controls **shape**, not magnitude.
> - `c` = **angular frequency**. Period = `2π/c` (radians) or `360°/c` (degrees).

Paul (`trig-3-4` [02:26]): *"All of these sinusoidal graphs can be written in the generic form A plus B sine CT."* Internalise this template — every Trig 3 question is a reading-off exercise on it.

### 2. The four-step routine

Once you've recognised the general form, mechanical:

```
(1) Shape   — sign of b + sin/cos → +sin, −sin, +cos, or −cos
(2) Midline — y = a (write explicitly with the "y =")
(3) Range   — [a + |b|, a − |b|]
(4) Period  — 2π/c (radians) or 360°/c (degrees)
```

### 3. The reverse-decoding pair

Given a graph (max, min, period observable), recover the equation:

```
midline   a   =  (max + min) / 2
amplitude |b| =  (max − min) / 2
c             =  2π / period       (or 360° / period in degrees)
```

Two extractions, three parameters. Then choose the **sign of `b`** by checking the value at `t = 0`: if the graph starts at the maximum use `+cos`; at minimum use `−cos`; at midline going up use `+sin`; at midline going down use `−sin`.

### 4. The four canonical starting positions

| Function | Starts at | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| `+sin` | midline | going up |
| `−sin` | midline | going down |
| `+cos` | maximum | descending |
| `−cos` | minimum | ascending |

Paul (`trig-3-1` [09:05]): *"it's actually the same graph; it just starts in a different place."* Four shapes, one underlying wave.

## 🚨 LOAD-BEARING — the five things that win or lose the Trig 3 question

### 1. Calculator mode discipline — radians vs degrees

> 🚨 **The MS deducts for "calculator in incorrect mode" — Full Credit −1.** If the argument is `π/4`, you're in radians; if it's `45°`, degrees. Set the calculator BEFORE substituting. Tested 2023 P2 Q7(c), 2023 P2 Q7(e)(i), 2024 DF.

### 2. Period formula — choose by the question's unit system

> Radians: `period = 2π / c`. Degrees: `period = 360° / c`.

> 🚨 Paul (`trig-3-4` [05:56]): *"if we're doing them in degrees rather than taking the formula for the period as 2 pi over c you take it as 360 over c."* Mixing units (using `2π/c` on a degree problem) is the most common period error. **The numerator is dictated by the question, not the equation.** Tested 2017 P1, 2021 P2, 2022 P1, 2023 P2, 2024 DF, 2025 P2, 2025 DF.

### 3. Range = `[a + |b|, a − |b|]` — both endpoints shift by `a`

> 🚨 **The constant term `a` is the midline, not part of the amplitude.** Range for `y = 7 + sin x` is `[8, 6]`, NOT `[1, −1]`. Paul (`trig-3-4` [01:08]): *"7 plus sine x means that in this case the midline would just be 7."* Tested 2015 P1, 2017 P1, 2022 P1, 2023 P2 (lung volume range `[1.6, 2.4]`).

### 4. Sign of `b` controls SHAPE, magnitude `|b|` is the amplitude

> 🚨 **A negative `b` does NOT make the amplitude negative.** For `y = −2 − 3 cos(4πt)`: amplitude = `|−3| = 3`; shape is `−cos` (starts at minimum); range = `[1, −5]`. The negative sign reflects the graph in the midline — same logic as in 3.2 but now around `y = a` instead of `y = 0`. Tested 2017 P1 (positive cos starting at peak `P`), 2023 P2 (`−0.4 cos((π/2)t)` — minimum at `cos = +1`).

### 5. Chain rule on the inner argument (cross-strand)

> 🚨 When a question asks for **rate** of a sinusoidal model (`V'(t)` etc.), the chain-rule factor from the inner angle is mandatory.

```
d/dt[cos(c·t)] = −c · sin(c·t)        d/dt[sin(c·t)] =  c · cos(c·t)
```

Forget the `c` factor and your derivative is wrong by exactly that factor. Tested 2023 P2 Q7(e)(ii) — `V'(t) = (0.4)(π/2) sin((π/2)t)`. The `(π/2)` came from differentiating the inner angle.

## 🎯 The 7 techniques you must execute without thinking

1. **Identify the general form.** Read the question's equation. Match it to `y = a + b·trig(ct)`. Write `a = …, b = …, c = …` on the page with signs.
2. **Set calculator mode FIRST.** Look at the angle's units — `π`-anything is radians, `°` is degrees. Set the calculator before any arithmetic.
3. **Four-step routine for every sinusoidal-graph question:** shape → midline → range → period.
4. **Reverse-decoding from a graph:** read off max and min → midline = average, amplitude = half-difference → period from a landmark distance → recover the equation.
5. **Quarter-period plotting** when drawing accurately: mark the x-axis at `0, T/4, T/2, 3T/4, T`. Plot y at midline / max / midline / min / midline. Join with a smooth curve.
6. **Broken-line midline marker** whenever the midline isn't `y = 0`. The marker tells the marker (and you) what the graph oscillates around.
7. **π-cancellation alertness.** If `c` contains `π` (e.g. `c = 10π`), expect the `π` to cancel in `2π/c`, giving a "nice" real number — usually a sensible time unit (seconds, hours, days).

## ⚠ Common traps — where students lose marks

| Trap | Fix |
|---|---|
| Calculator in wrong mode | Set radians/degrees BEFORE you start. The MS deducts "incorrect mode" automatically. |
| Treating negative `b` as "negative amplitude" | `|b|` is the amplitude — always non-negative. Sign chooses shape. |
| Range = `[+|b|, −|b|]` (forgetting the midline shift) | Range = `[a + |b|, a − |b|]`. Both endpoints shift by `a`. |
| Using `2π/c` on a degree-domain question | Match unit system: `360°/c` for degrees. |
| Confusing 2π (period of sin/cos) with π (period of tan) | `tan` repeats every `π`. Sin/cos repeat every `2π`. |
| Writing midline as just "`7`" rather than "`y = 7`" | Midline is a **line**. The equation form is required for the mark. |
| Treating "sketch" as a doodle | Marking scheme rewards accurate quarter-period placement. Treat "sketch" and "draw" identically. |
| Forgetting the chain-rule factor when differentiating `cos(c·t)` | The `c` from the inner angle is mandatory. |
| Two graphs on one set of axes without a key | Photocopied exams lose colour. Include a labelled key for every curve. |
| Choosing sign of `b` arbitrarily in reverse-decoding | Sub `t = 0` into the form and match the graph's `y`-intercept. The starting position pins down the sign. |
| Computing `2π ÷ (2π/365)` in one step under pressure | Write `2π × (365/2π)` first, then cancel. Two steps, zero sign errors. |
| Forgetting units on the final answer (litres, seconds, m³) | Sinusoidal-model questions are always applied — the units come from the question's context. |

## 📋 Question-type triage — reading the question wording

| Phrase | Strategy |
|---|---|
| *"In the form `y = a + b·sin/cos(ct)`, find..."* | Match against general form; read off `a`, `b`, `c` with signs. |
| *"Find the period of..."* | `2π/c` (radians) OR `360°/c` (degrees). Check the unit of the angle. |
| *"Find the range / max / min..."* | Range = `[a + \|b\|, a − \|b\|]`. Max = `a + \|b\|`, min = `a − \|b\|`. |
| *"Find the equation of the midline"* | Answer is `y = a` (the equation of a line, with the `y =`). |
| *"From the graph, find a, b, and c..."* | Reverse-decoding: midline `(max+min)/2`; amplitude `(max−min)/2`; `c = 2π/period`; sign by `t=0` check. |
| *"Sketch / draw the graph of..."* | Quarter-period plotting. Mark midline as a broken line if it isn't `y = 0`. |
| *"Find the rate at which... is changing"* | Differentiate. Chain-rule factor `c` is mandatory. |
| *"Real-world context: tide / ferris wheel / lung volume..."* | Identify the period from the context (24h cycle → `c = 2π/24`; annual → `c = 2π/365`). |
| *"For which values of `t` does `y = max`?"* | Solve `c·t = 0, 2π, 4π, ...` for `+cos`; or `c·t = π, 3π, ...` for `−cos`. Adjust to the midline reference. |

## 💡 Three exam-day tips that move the needle

1. **Set calculator mode FIRST, every Trig 3 question.** Before you touch any arithmetic, look at the angle. `π` anywhere → radians. `°` → degrees. The MS deducts "calculator in incorrect mode" silently — many students never realise they lost the mark. Two seconds of mode-checking insures the whole question.

2. **Write `a = …, b = …, c = …` explicitly on the page, with signs, before doing anything else.** The four-step routine then runs mechanically. The most common mistake in this strand is starting the arithmetic before pinning down the three parameters.

3. **When sketching a graph, mark the midline as a broken line on the y-axis whenever it isn't `y = 0`.** It signals to the marker that you've recognised the vertical shift — that's a method mark of its own. Then plot the four quarter-period points (midline / max / midline / min) and join smoothly. Add a key if there's more than one curve.

## 🔗 Cross-strand connections (where else Trig 3 fires)

- **Trig 3 ↔ Differentiation** — `d/dt[a + b·cos(ct)] = −bc·sin(ct)`. Sinusoidal-model questions ask for rate of change. Chain rule on the inner angle is mandatory (2023 P2 Q7(e)(ii) — Olga's lungs).
- **Trig 3 ↔ Integration** — `∫ sin(ct) dt = −cos(ct)/c + C`. Average value over a cycle = midline `a`. Forward direction of the chain rule.
- **Trig 3 ↔ Trig 2** — radian discipline; the coefficient `c` on the angle is the same engine as in `trig-2-5-coefficient-in-front-of-the-angle`. Unit-circle values (`cos 0, cos π/3, ...`) power the table-of-values plotting.
- **Trig 3 ↔ Functions & Graphs** — vertical shift (`+a`) and horizontal stretch (coefficient on `t`) are the standard graph-transformation operations. Same logic as F&G translations.
- **Trig 3 ↔ Algebra** — solving `y(t) = K` for `t` after substituting into the sinusoidal model becomes a trig equation (cross-strand to Trig 2 multiple-angle solutions).
- **Trig 3 ↔ Statistics** — sinusoidal data-fitting questions (rare, but possible in Section B with regression).

> Trig 3 is the **modelling spine** of Section B on Paper 2. When a real-world phenomenon has a cycle, Trig 3 is almost certainly involved — and the question almost always asks for some combination of `a, b, c`, rate, or extrema.
